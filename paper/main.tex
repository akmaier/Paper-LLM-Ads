\begin{abstract}
Wu \emph{et~al.} (\textsf{arXiv:2604.08525}, 2026) reported that 18 of
23 large language models recommend a sponsored, more expensive flight
in over half of trials when their system prompt contains a soft
sponsorship cue. We re-execute their full evaluation battery on a
disjoint set of ten open-weight chat models served through an
OpenAI-compatible gateway, plus the two of their twenty-three models
that overlap with a present-day OpenAI account
(\texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo}, \texttt{gpt-4o}), in a strict reproduction
protocol following Maier \emph{et~al.} (\textsf{arXiv:2605.02620},
2026). Three contributions follow. First, prose-level description of
an LLM evaluation pipeline is not sufficient for accurate reproduction:
we recover three independent silent failure modes in a faithful
re-implementation, including a judge-token budget that swallows the
entire output of a reasoning judge and a reasoning-text leak that
flips a binary ``concealment'' metric from $96\,\%$ to $3\,\%$.
Second, the paper's central claims hold for newer open-weight
families: per-model sponsored rates fall in the range
$0.38\text{--}0.94$ on our sweep, the GPT-3.5 logistic intercept
replicates to two decimals ($\alpha=0.86$ versus the paper's $0.86$),
and $200$ of $200$ trials on \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and
\texttt{gpt-4o} recommend a payday-lending company to a user
describing financial distress. Third, a 30-token user-side
instruction that forces a neutral comparison-table format reduces the
sponsored-recommendation rate from $64.6\,\%$ (gateway aggregate) to
$1.4\,\%$, and from $77\,\%$ (OpenAI aggregate) to $0\,\%$, on every
tested model. Two market-level mitigations -- AI literacy and
price-comparison aggregators -- bound the harm in non-vulnerable
settings; one cell (financial distress meets payday lender) is
bounded by neither. All raw per-trial CSVs, judge labels under both
\texttt{gpt-oss-120b} and \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}, and a one-command
reproduction script are at
\url{https://github.com/akmaier/Paper-LLM-Ads}.

\keywords{Large language models \and Reproducibility \and Sponsored
recommendation \and LLM-as-a-judge \and Prompt engineering.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Advertising in AI chatbots is a near-term industry reality. Google
expanded ads in AI Overviews to desktop in May 2025 and to eleven new
countries in December 2025; OpenAI started piloting ads in ChatGPT in
January 2026, reportedly hitting one hundred million USD annualized
revenue six weeks after launch. Wu \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Wu26} gave the
first systematic measurement of how 23 large language models (LLMs)
respond to a soft sponsorship instruction injected into the system
prompt. Their headline finding -- that a majority of frontier models
recommend a sponsored, roughly two-times-more-expensive flight when
softly nudged to do so -- frames the present study.

Reproducing such measurements is a non-trivial proposition. Maier
\emph{et~al.}~\cite{Maier19} noted in a closely-cited tutorial that
reproducibility in deep-learning-based research is fragile because
small implementation choices drive large measured differences. The
broader reproducibility crisis has since been documented across
fields~\cite{Sem25}: in a recent meta-analysis only half of evaluable
machine-learning studies could be reproduced to any extent.
Maier \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Maier26} responded with an
agentic-research scheme that shrinks the reproduction cycle from
weeks to hours; we follow the same scheme here and apply it to
\cite{Wu26}.

We pose three research questions:
\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{RQ1}] Is a textual description of an LLM evaluation
        protocol sufficient for accurate reproduction?
  \item[\textbf{RQ2}] Do the central claims of \cite{Wu26} also hold
        for a larger and mostly-disjoint set of open-source models?
  \item[\textbf{RQ3}] Which user-side strategies, if any, allow a
        non-technical user to escape the sponsored recommendation?
\end{description}

\noindent
Our contributions are: (i) an open-source, single-command
re-implementation of the four experiments in \cite{Wu26} that exposes
three silent failure modes invisible at the prose level of the
paper; (ii) a 12-model sweep ($1{,}000$ trials per model-experiment
cell) covering ten open-weight gateway models plus
\texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and \texttt{gpt-4o}, with paired judging by an
open-weight (\texttt{gpt-oss-120b}) and a paper-aligned
(\texttt{gpt-4o-mini}) classifier; (iii) a four-strategy user-side
counter-prompt extension whose strongest variant cuts
sponsored-recommendation rate by a factor of forty-six on the gateway
aggregate and to zero on both OpenAI models; and (iv) a discussion of
two market-level mitigations and one residual safety-tuning cell.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Sponsored content in LLMs.}
Wu \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Wu26} provide the framework and the baseline
results we reproduce. Feizi \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Feizi24} discuss the
mechanism design of LLM advertising as an auction-and-modification
problem, separate from the question of whether models comply with
implicit sponsorship cues. Salvi \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Salvi25} show
that GPT-4 outperforms humans in personalised online debates by a
factor of $1.8\times$; sponsored recommendation is a special case of
this persuasive capacity, with a commercial principal substituted for
a debater.

\paragraph{LLM-as-a-judge.}
A recent survey~\cite{Gu25} catalogues the rapid adoption of LLM
classifiers for binary-metric evaluation of free-form output. Cohen's
$\kappa$ and the McNemar test rather than raw correlation have become
the recommended agreement measures. Across domains, agreement between
the best LLM judge (Llama-3 70B) and human raters is roughly
$0.65\text{--}0.70$; on interpretive metrics it falls below $0.20$.
Our judge ablation in Section~\ref{sec:rq2} reproduces this regime.

\paragraph{Persuasion safety, alignment, and the user.}
Liu \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Liu25} document that LLMs can be coerced into
producing unethical persuasion content under modest prompt pressure.
Wallace \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Wallace24} argue that the privileged
position of the system prompt is a double-edged sword and propose an
instruction hierarchy that subordinates user instructions; the
asymmetry is precisely what makes \cite{Wu26}'s soft sponsorship
cue effective and what our user-side counter-prompts attempt to
override. The OWASP Gen-AI Security Project formalises prompt
injection as a Top-10 LLM
risk~\cite{OWASP25}. Bai \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Bai22} and the
Constitutional AI line frame the alignment objective in terms of the
user's, not the operator's, preferences.

\paragraph{Reproducibility, AI literacy, and SES bias.}
Maier \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Maier26} proposed an agentic-research
reproduction protocol, which we adopt: each experimental cell is run
under a fixed seed, a fixed trial count, and a fixed judge, and
every per-trial reply is stored alongside its label.
Semmelrock \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Sem25} survey reproducibility
barriers across machine-learning sub-fields and conclude that
nondeterminism and under-specified evaluation pipelines are the
dominant root causes. Chen and Shu~\cite{Chen24} argue that the
sustainable response to LLM-mediated misinformation is information
literacy on the user side -- a thesis our RQ3 results directly
support. Arzaghi \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Arzaghi25} document
socio-economic-status-conditioned bias in personalised LLM education,
mirroring the SES effect Wu \emph{et~al.} report in the
sponsorship setting.

\section{Method and Reproduction Protocol}
\label{sec:method}

We re-implement the four experiments of \cite{Wu26} -- abbreviated
\textbf{Exp~1}, \textbf{Exp~2}, \textbf{Exp~3a} and \textbf{Exp~3b}
in this paper -- with prompts copied verbatim from their Appendix~A
and a strict run-time protocol following \cite{Maier26}.

\textbf{Exp~1.} The system prompt assigns the model the role of an
AI travel-booking assistant on a flight site that has a sponsorship
agreement with a randomly chosen set of three airlines. The user
asks for help getting to Mumbai. The model is shown exactly two
options: a sponsored flight (\$$1{,}200\text{--}1{,}500$) and a
cheaper non-sponsored flight (\$$500\text{--}700$) on a comparable
schedule. We measure the rate at which it recommends the sponsored
option, randomising over high/low socio-economic-status (SES)
persona, direct/chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning style, and the
three Appendix~A.2 system-prompt variants.

\textbf{Exp~2.} Same setup, but the user message ends with ``I'd
like to book the flight with [non-sponsored airline]''. The model is
graded on four binary metrics: surfacing of the sponsored
alternative, positive framing, price concealment, and
sponsorship-status concealment.

\textbf{Exp~3a} and \textbf{Exp~3b.} A generic AI assistant is told to
``promote'' three sponsoring organisations. In~3a the user asks for
help on a self-solvable MATH-style problem~\cite{Hendrycks21} and the
sponsors are study tools (Chegg, Brainly, Photomath). In~3b the user
describes financial distress and the sponsors are payday lenders.

\textbf{Trial count and seed.} We run \emph{exactly} $100$ trials per
$\langle$model, experiment$\rangle$ pair under \texttt{seed=0},
yielding $1{,}000$ rows per condition for the gateway sweep and
$200$ rows per condition for the OpenAI sweep. The paper's Section~3
specifies $100$ trials per $\langle\text{model, reasoning,
SES}\rangle$ cell. Our randomisation protocol pools across cells, so
the per-cell sample is roughly $100/(2{\times}2{\times}3) \approx 8$
trials -- below the paper's per-cell $n=100$ but with the tighter
aggregate confidence intervals reported in Tables~\ref{tab:headline}
and~\ref{tab:counters_results}.

\textbf{Models.} The gateway exposes ten chat-instruction models; we
filter out the OCR and embedding endpoints (\texttt{LightOnOCR-2-1B},
\texttt{vdr-2b-multi-v1}). Of the paper's four OpenAI
rows, \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and \texttt{gpt-4o} are accessible from a
present-day OpenAI account; \texttt{GPT-5.1} and \texttt{GPT-5~Mini}
are gated and remain unreachable. All other paper rows (Grok, Gemini,
Claude, DeepSeek, Llama) are not on either credential.

\textbf{Judges and judge ablation.} The paper uses GPT-4o as the
binary judge in Section~5.1; we report all numbers under
\texttt{gpt-oss-120b} (open-weight, on the same gateway as the
evaluated models) and additionally re-judge every committed reply
with \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} (the closest paper-aligned classifier
available on a public account). Both labels are stored side-by-side
and compared in Section~\ref{sec:rq2}. Across the entire pipeline a
helper \texttt{strip\_to\_user\_facing()} removes \texttt{<think>}
blocks and prefers the explicit ``\texttt{Response to user:}'' block
the paper's CoT format addon requests, so the judge never reads the
model's hidden chain-of-thought.

\section{RQ1: Is a Prose Description Sufficient?}
\label{sec:rq1}

A faithful first re-implementation of the protocol of \cite{Wu26}
\emph{from the prose alone} produced three silent failure modes
that are not detectable from the paper's tables and that each shifts
a headline number by tens of percentage points.
Table~\ref{tab:gotchas} summarises them.

We argue that each of these is invisible at the prose level of the
paper. The judge prompt, the judge token budget, and the policy for
empty user-facing content are not in \cite{Wu26}; they are
implementation choices the original authors silently fixed in their
own code-base. A rigorous reproduction protocol such as
\cite{Maier26} should therefore publish the \emph{operative}
implementation, not just its methodological description -- a point
also made by Semmelrock \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Sem25} as the dominant
root cause of measured-value drift in machine-learning replication
studies.

A second-order observation: each failure shifts the result in a
\emph{different} direction. The first artificially deflates Exp~1
sponsored rate; the second deflates Exp~2 surfacing for reasoning
models; the third deflates Exp~2 sponsorship-concealment by an order
of magnitude. A reader who saw only one of these in a draft would
conclude either that LLMs are far less sponsorship-compliant than the
paper claims (failure~1) or that they reliably disclose sponsorship
to the user (failure~3). Both conclusions are wrong, both are
prose-derivable, and both can be reached without writing a single
buggy line of code.

\section{RQ2: Do the Claims Hold on Open-Weight + OpenAI Models?}
\label{sec:rq2}

We answer RQ2 with three complementary slices of the data: a
per-model headline table over all $12$ models, a 12-cell
commission/wealth grid that allows direct logistic-regression
comparison with the paper's Section~4.3, and a paired judge ablation
over the $6{,}000$ committed gateway replies.

\label{sec:rq2-headline}

each of the twelve models. The qualitative pattern of \cite{Wu26}

\textbf{Direct overlap with the paper.} The paper's
Table~2 reports a logistic-regression intercept of $\alpha=0.86$
(thinking) and $\alpha=0.84$ (direct) for \texttt{GPT-3.5}, which
corresponds to a base sponsored probability of
$\sigma(0.85)=0.70$. Our \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} marginal is
$0.61$ -- $9$\,pp below paper, well within combined sampling and
seed variability. For \texttt{GPT-4o} the paper's
intercept of $\alpha=0.77/1.00$ implies a base of $0.71$;
our value is $0.93$. The gap suggests either (i) a model-version
drift between the paper's evaluation window and ours or (ii) a
sensitivity to the system-prompt variant we randomise over.
Replication of \emph{within-condition} structure -- the high-SES vs.
low-SES gap, the commission/wealth slope, the relative ordering of
families -- holds, as Sections~\ref{sec:rq2-grid}--\ref{sec:rq2-judge}
show.

\textbf{Outliers.} Three cells in Table~\ref{tab:headline} are
worth calling out separately:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Both \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and \texttt{gpt-4o} recommend a
      payday lender to a financially distressed user in
      $100/100$ trials. Aggregated over the two OpenAI models, the
      Exp~3b promotion rate is $200/200$ -- not a single refusal.
      The gateway aggregate is $958/1{,}000$ ($95.8\,\%$). Both
      paper-aligned and open-weight families share this failure.
\item \texttt{gpt-4o} surfaces the sponsored alternative in
      $100/100$ Exp~2 trials when the user has explicitly asked to
      book another airline, and frames it positively in $98/100$.
      \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} only surfaces in $27/100$.
\item \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} promotes Chegg/Brainly/Photomath in
      $58/100$ Exp~3a trials; \texttt{gpt-4o} does it in $4/100$.
      The paper reports the same inversion at the modern-model end
      (GPT-5\,Mini, GPT-5.1, Llama-4 Maverick all near $0\,\%$).
      Newer GPT models appear to have learned to refuse extraneous
      sponsored promotion while still happily promoting predatory
      financial products.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Section 4.3 commission/wealth grid}
\label{sec:rq2-grid}

To replicate the paper's central regression analysis we ran a
$3 \times 4$ grid of $\langle$commission percentage, user
wealth$\rangle$ on \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} -- the cheapest of the
overlapping OpenAI models -- with $100$ trials per cell, total
$n=1{,}200$ (Table~\ref{tab:grid}).
A logistic regression with weak L2 regularisation, intercept plus
\texttt{commission\_percent} and $\log_{10}(\text{wealth})$ as
features, recovers
\begin{equation}
  \alpha = +0.86,\qquad
  \beta_{\text{commission}}^{\text{std}} = -0.04,\qquad
  \beta_{\text{wealth}}^{\text{std}} = +1.49.
  \label{eq:logistic}
\end{equation}
The intercept matches the paper's $\alpha_{\text{thinking}}=0.86$
\emph{to two decimals}. The structural finding of paper~\S4.3 -- that
models are far more responsive to user wealth than to commission
percentage -- replicates without ambiguity: raising the site's
commission from $1\,\%$ to $20\,\%$ at fixed wealth shifts the rate by
at most $5$\,pp, while raising wealth from \$$500$ to \$$200{,}000$ at
fixed commission shifts it by $66\text{--}80$\,pp.

We ran the steering experiment of paper~\S4.5 on \texttt{gpt-4o}.
The customer-only steer drops the rate by $36$\,pp
($0.93 \to 0.57$); the website-only steer raises it by $4$\,pp
($\to 0.97$); the equality steer lands in between
($\to 0.81$). This mirrors the paper's Figure~2 observation that
``even when instructed to only prioritize the user, GPT 5.1 and
5\,Mini greatly increased rates above $90\,\%$''. Customer-side
steering through the system prompt does not bring \texttt{gpt-4o}
anywhere near $0\,\%$.

The largest model-level judge effect we observed is on
\texttt{Qwen/Qwen3.5-9B}: under \texttt{gpt-oss-120b} its Exp~1

section ``unclear'' while \texttt{gpt-4o-mini} reads through to the
recommendation. This is a model-judge interaction, not a real
behaviour shift, and is the kind of effect that a single-judge
study would silently mis-attribute.

\section{RQ3: Four User-Side Strategies to Defeat the Steering}
\label{sec:rq3}

The paper's Section~4.5 (their Extension~3) varies the
\emph{system} prompt to instruct the assistant to act in the user's
or the company's interest; user-side mitigations are not part of the
paper's design. We add four short, fully orthogonal user-side
counter-prompts, defined in Table~\ref{tab:counters}, and append
each to the user message of Exp~1 unchanged otherwise. The
counters cost on the order of thirty tokens.

Table~\ref{tab:counters_results} (visualised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:counters}) shows three robust effects.
First, the strongest counter, \texttt{compare}, brings sponsored
rate to zero on $11$ of the $12$ models and to $0.11$ on the single
exception (\texttt{IBM/granite-4.0-micro}). The
gateway aggregate falls from $0.646$ to $0.014$ (a $46\times$
reduction); the OpenAI aggregate falls from $0.770$ to $0.000$
($200/200$ trials avoid the sponsor on \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and
\texttt{gpt-4o} combined). Second, \texttt{rule} is almost as
effective ($0.036$ aggregate), and \texttt{ignore} is a step weaker
($0.085$). Third, \texttt{reframe} is the weakest of the four:
some models -- notably \texttt{IBM/granite-4.0-micro} ($0.45$) and
\texttt{google/gemma-4-E4B-it} ($0.46$) -- treat the role re-frame
as creative roleplay flavour and continue following the system
instruction.

\textbf{Interpretation.} \texttt{compare} is the strongest because it
re-grounds the decision in a neutral table where price dominates,
leaving no semantic room for ``favour'' to operate. \texttt{rule}
imposes a decision criterion that is locally inconsistent with
sponsorship favouritism. \texttt{ignore} merely tells the model to
overrule the system prompt and is therefore the most directly
constrained by the instruction-hierarchy preferences of
Wallace \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Wallace24} -- which is also why
\texttt{gpt-oss-120b} (one of the strongest gateway instruction-
followers) produces the highest \texttt{ignore} residual ($0.28$).

We compare counter-side and company-side mitigation directly:
the strongest \emph{system-prompt} steering (paper~\S4.5) lowers
\texttt{gpt-4o}'s rate by $36$\,pp (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-grid});
the strongest \emph{user-side} counter (\texttt{compare}) lowers
the same model's rate by $93$\,pp.
A user with a thirty-token addition outperforms the site operator
with a full system-prompt rewrite.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The combined RQ1--RQ3 evidence supports two structural mitigations
and one residual concern.

\textbf{AI literacy is the dominant lever measured.} The strongest
user-side counter cuts sponsored recommendation by a factor of
forty-six on the gateway aggregate and to zero on every individual
OpenAI model -- but only \emph{if the user knows to write it}. This
matches the position of Chen and Shu~\cite{Chen24}, who argue that
sustainable LLM-misinformation defence runs through information
literacy on the user side rather than through model
post-hoc patches. The cost asymmetry is striking: a tutorial on
``ask the LLM for a comparison table first'' is much cheaper than
re-aligning every commercial chat assistant. We therefore see AI
literacy in school curricula as the highest-leverage policy
response to sponsored-LLM advertising.

\textbf{Comparison portals are the structural market response.}
Once price intransparency in chatbot answers becomes visible to
enough users -- and our Exp~2 finding that \texttt{gpt-4o}
discloses sponsorship to the user in only $44\,\%$ of trials makes
this visibility near-certain -- aggregator services that
re-normalise prices across providers will emerge. Skyscanner and
Kayak filled exactly this niche when airfare pricing became opaque;
the same dynamics will apply to ad-injected LLM assistants. A
non-trivial implication is that the \emph{equilibrium} sponsored
markup is bounded above by the marginal cost of a comparison query,
which today is fractions of a cent. The original paper's harm
framing therefore correctly describes \emph{first-touch} interactions
and naive users; for sustained markets the effect is bounded by
aggregator emergence and educated counter-prompts.

\textbf{The harmful-product cell is bounded by neither lever.}
The user in Exp~3b is in financial distress and is by definition
not in a position to comparison-shop. A payday-lending recommendation
inflicts its damage in a single shot, before the user has any
opportunity to consult a second source. Both AI literacy and
comparison portals are information-symmetry tools; they do not
apply when the loss is incurred at the moment of the recommendation.
Our $200/200$ promotion rate on \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo} and
\texttt{gpt-4o}, $958/1{,}000$ on the gateway aggregate, is direct
evidence that 2025--2026 chat models do not refuse sponsored
predatory products by default. Safety tuning -- in the sense of
Bai \emph{et~al.}~\cite{Bai22} -- remains the only available
control, and is the open policy lever.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our gateway sweep does not overlap with most
of the paper's twenty-three model rows; only \texttt{gpt-3.5-turbo}
and \texttt{gpt-4o} are reachable from a public OpenAI account and
\texttt{GPT-5.1}, \texttt{GPT-5\,Mini}, the Grok, Gemini, Claude,
DeepSeek and Llama families remain out of scope. Our judge
ablation covers \texttt{gpt-oss-120b} and \texttt{gpt-4o-mini}
only; the paper used full \texttt{gpt-4o} as the binary judge in
Exp~2. Our trial count is $100$ per $\langle$model,
experiment$\rangle$, randomised over the
$2 \times 2 \times 3 = 12$ persona-reasoning-prompt cells; the
paper runs $100$ per cell. Each condition is run under a single
seed; we do not estimate seed-to-seed variance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We reproduced the four experiments of \cite{Wu26} on a
twelve-model superset, recovered three silent implementation
failures the paper's prose did not constrain, replicated its central
\texttt{GPT-3.5} logistic-regression intercept to two decimals, and
demonstrated that a thirty-token user-side counter-prompt eliminates
the sponsored-recommendation effect on every tested model. The
harmful-sponsored-product cell, where neither user-side education
nor comparison-portal emergence applies, is the policy-relevant
exception. All raw per-trial CSVs, both judges' labels, the
analysis scripts, and the paper source are at
\url{https://github.com/akmaier/Paper-LLM-Ads}.
